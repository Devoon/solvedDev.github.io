Characteristics of Characters:

Holden

- Main character of the story.
- Immature, feisty, always doing something stupid (terrible mistakes, getting in a fight, etc.).
- Not willing to let go of the protection of his childhood and move on.
- Greatly mourns the loss of his brother, Allie.
- Is very spontaneous (he never thinks things out in advance).
- Very Observative.


Phoebe

- Holden's younger sister.
- Was to be given a record of Little Shirley Beans, but then that record shattered.
- Person most trusted by Holden.
- Energetic, fun, always wanting to do something.
- Considerably more mature than the childish Holden.


Allie

- Not actually in the story.
- Really important to Holden's character.
- His death is what perpetuated Holden into depression.
- Holden’s younger brother.
- His baseball mitt has several poems on it. Holden reads them often, and shows them to (basically) no one.

Main Idea and Theme:


Main Idea

The main idea of the story is of a character with a painful past overcoming it. The whole story is of Holden’s getting over his brother’s death. This is the painful past. The overcoming of it comes with the rest of the story. The entire story sees him messing around, never thinking of the future (and when he does, its very idealistic), and always childish. He never grew up till the last handful of chapters. The story follows his growth as a character, with him growing up, especially with the last five or so chapters. Once Holden meets Phoebe, Phoebe brings reality back to him. He got kicked out, and he needs to do something about it. Him handing over his hunting hat at the end symbolized him finally completely realizing that he needed to grow up, and so he did. He is leaving the protections of childhood, his hat, in Phoebe's care.


Theme

The theme of the story is the harsh, painfulness of growing up. In the story, as mentioned within the main idea, Holden does not want to grow up. He stays away from his home, he stays away from his maturity, instead vying to stick with his childish nature. This does not help him. He doesn’t grow up, he never grows up (until the end), all because of Allie’s death, but once he does grow up, he’s freed. He gets over his harsh past, over all his wrongdoings, over getting kicked out, and decides that he needs to just take a step forward in life. It may be hard and hurt at first, but that’s what he has to do, and that’s why it’s the theme. His growth and ultimate decision all swayed with the fact that he had to grow up, and the fact that he knew it. It also shows his growth and decision to do such an action, and the positive outcomes of it, almost creating a sort of propaganda for growing up, through all the hardships and pain.


Ten Points of the Novel:


1

- Holden’s hunting hat, which he (almost) always wears.


2

- The Little Shirley Beans Record (the gift to be, before it wasn’t).


3

- Holden wondering where the ducks have gone, whether it be in the cab, while talking, while listening, or while doing anything else.


4

- Mr. Spencer and his speech to Holden (which Holden was both not listening too and making fun of (at different points of it)).


5

- Holden losing the Fencing team’s equipment, which he thinks is pretty funny, but the Fencing team says otherwise.


6

- Allie’s baseball mitt and its several poems written all over it (and its emotional value to Holden).


7

- Holden and Stradlater’s short-winded fight over Jane Gallagher and her possible actions with Stradlater (acted as a way to vent Holden’s steam, and that’s basically it).


8

- Holden’s trip to the museum, which brought up several memories, all about Phoebe and his classmates.


9

- Holden’s trip to the skating rink with Sally, and the terrible falling that followed.


10

- Holden visiting Phoebe, and her eventual explosion after realizing that he got kicked out.
